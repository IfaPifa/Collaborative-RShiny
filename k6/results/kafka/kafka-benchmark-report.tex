\section{Analysis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The Kafka architecture passes 6 of 9 benchmarks. The three failures share a root cause:
the R Shiny consumer loop (\texttt{consumer\$consume(100)} inside \texttt{reactivePoll(100\,ms)})
is single-threaded and processes messages sequentially. Under concurrent load, the consumer
falls behind, causing poll latency to dominate total relay time.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Relay latency} (01): The POST to Kafka is fast (avg 46.6\,ms), but the
        poll-until-visible step averages 5.7\,s because the R consumer must drain its
        backlog before the new message appears in Redis.
  \item \textbf{Collaboration propagation} (02): Same mechanism — the guest's GET sees
        stale state until the consumer catches up (avg 6.73\,s).
  \item \textbf{Multi-user scaling} (08): With 10 concurrent guests polling, the first
        guest sees the update in 39\,ms (already in Redis), but the last guest waits
        up to 36.7\,s due to consumer lag compounding across apps.
  \item \textbf{App-specific failures}: Analytics and MonteCarlo consistently fail relay
        checks. These apps have heavier R processing (dataset joins, simulation loops),
        which slows their consumer further.
\end{itemize}

Benchmarks that bypass the Kafka consumer path (save/restore via PostgreSQL, session
lifecycle via Spring Boot, WebSocket presence via STOMP) perform well, confirming the
bottleneck is isolated to the Kafka $\to$ R consumer $\to$ Redis pipeline.
